What isn't Scrum?

Common misconceptions

  • We're not talking about rugby.
  • You can't "do a scrum"
  • You are never "in the process of scrumming"

  • We're talking about building things, not dancing in the mud.
  • Scrum, unlike rugby, isn't a game with a strict set of rules, it's a mindset that can be adapted to suit your business.
  • Scrumming isn't a word, so just don't do that.

Scrum is...

A methodology that allows each team member to work concurrently, in a horizontal hierachical structure, that allows each member of the team to get on with their job.

In English, Scrum is a tool that lets you figure out how to get all of your staff to work on the project all at the same time. No more waiting, no more struggling with chinese whispers as work is passed down the chain.

In the programming world, there are broadly two ways to build a program.

You can compile a program from source every time you make a change. This requires you to hope and pray that you didn't make any errors, because you won't find out until you compile, and that might take 5-10 minutes if you've got a big project. If you're not a very experienced programmer, that's a lot of wasted time.

However there are other lanaguages which support something called REPL. These allow you to conduct small tests on things you are unsure about and receive an instant response. This helps speed up development, which makes everyone happier.

Cool people call traditional project management practices "waterfall"

In the project management world, traditional (waterfall) practices have their own sort of "compile time", where the requirements are set, a wheel is set in motion to build to the requirements.

What's wrong with this workflow (ask audience)?

  • Coders, designers, testers never speak to the people making requirements
  • Analysis never speak to coders
  • Testers never speak to analysts
  • Chinese whispers

Waterfall is a compiler. Made out of people.

AKA, the worst idea ever.

Scrum is a project management REPL

Product backlog is a list of tasks you need to do. You break up big chunks into smaller tasks, and then prioritise them.

We'll talk about how to do this the scrum way later.

Once we have a backlog, we decide what work we will include into the sprint, and in which order. These tasks will be taken out of the product backlog and are locked into the timeline.

The sprint then occurs (normally it's a 2 week thing in software, ignore this diagram), which is when the team work to complete the tasks.

Every day there's a feedback loop coming from daily short standup meetings (standups) which help the whole team communicate progress & barriers.

The mindset of scrum is to always be shipping, so all sprints should end up with a stable, shippable product.

    The Scrum Wheel of productiveness

    Two things:

    • Review what's up next in the backlog, and how to achieve it. The product owner (previously known as the QA person), will be questioned to make sure the whole team understands the task, but also where it fits in with the entire project, and why.
    • The team then figure out how to achieve the task, and how long it'll take. They often use a scrum tool called planning poker to do this (more on this later).

    The result of the meeting is the sprint backlog.

    Standups

    • What did I accomplish yesterday?
    • What will I do today?
    • What's blocking me?

    Every day, have a meeting with the product. Stand up so it's not just mentally painful for everyone to faff on.

    Remember, Scrum is all about doing, not mucking about, so timebox the meeting to 5-15 minutes.

    Every team member should ask 3 things:

    • What did I accomplish Yesterday?
    • What will I do today?
    • What's blocking me?

    This is easy to implement in your company tomorrow. But don't let that be an excuse to just use this tool and nothing else.

    How do I measure how big a task is when I break it down?

    With planning poker of course!

    iOS: http://bit.ly/planningpokerios

    Android: http://bit.ly/planningpokerandroid

    Planning poker helps define how big a task is.

    • Find the smallest task on your backlog, and agree that the smallest task is a "1"
    • Find the largest task on your backlog, and agree that the largest task is a "50"
    • Therefore a 100 is double the size of a 50, and a 5 is 5 times as large as a 1

    I've missed stuff out.

    Because you probably don't work for Nokia, and your team probably isn't 200 strong.

    Classical Scrum needs 5-9 people to work. 7 is optimal.

    You will need:

    • A product owner
    • A Scrum Master
    • Some Developers
    • A Designer
    • A Tester

    Most startups don't have the cash to have a guy who's fulltime job is to be a product owner / devils advocate

    Most startups can't afford to have a developer who can't test his own code either (and if you have a developer that can't test his own code, please fire him as he is a liability.)

    In reality, a designer often doesn't need to be about for the entire duration of the product development cycle

    Different businesses will need different people, but they all boil down to makers, designers and QA

    The Scrum Master is usually also a maker or designer

    Classical Scrum has rules about how to scale past 7 people.

    In large companies, having 7 people to one project is not enough, so you have the concept of scrum-of-scrums. This allows you to scale the methodology to hundreds or even thousands of team members.

    You have a scrum of scrummasters who then delegate to the teams, keeping all the scrums in sync with each other.

    Classical Scrum says there's no hierarchy and that everyone is equal.

    If your company is small, this isn't always so easy to do - the CEO is probably in your scrum team and that might cause ranking problems.

    There's lots of other small meetings and other things you can have to make things more structured

    But sometimes too much structure causes confusion at the start
